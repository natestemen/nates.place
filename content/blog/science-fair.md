For the second year in the making I participated in the [Georgetown Steam Plant Science Fair](https://www.georgetownsteamplant.org/2025).


In 2024 and 2026 I had the honor of participating in the georgetown steam plant science fair.
You probably remember science fairs as a thing that students do in middle school, and this isn't so different, but most of the exhibitors are adults, and this science fair takes place inside a decommisioned steam plant built in 1905.
Somehow, my participation in this science fair made me an artist.

In 2024 I learned the steam plant was hosting a science fair from a flyer in Seattle's Georgetown neighborhood while having lunch with some friends.
On the website they had a call for participation across SHTEAM (Science, History, Technology, Engineering, Art, and Math).
Quite fitting for a steam plant.
I wrote up an application that would hopefully explain to people how randomness plays a crucial role in modern computing infrastructure, while also touching on fundamentals like 'what actually does it mean to be random' and 'does the randomness in quantum mechanics have something to say about free will'?
I intentionally chose a topic I knew enough about to make it happen, but not enough about it to truly feel comfortable about it.

Sure enough, the project was accepted, and what started as a month to create the project quickly procrastinated to a week.
I planned on creating a poster (like an 8th grade science project; BORING), but learned that there would be very limited to hang stuff on the wall (they gotta preserve the 1905 building after all).
I'd perused the second use building supply store enough to know they have lots of interesting material, so i popped down there and quickly realized an old door would be a great replacement for a poster.
The next week i spent the after work hours frantically putting together ideas into a sort of boardgame style adventure through randomness.
I planned on giving people a short lecture, and then showing a simple procedure to generate random numbers, generating some random art, then talking about quantum mechanics and asking them if they thought they had free will.
It sounds ridiculous writing it all down, but i was excited and was not effectively considering who my audience would be.
The first day, shortly after setting up my door, two parents and their 7 year old child came up to check our my project.
I talked about random permutations of cards, and generated some number with them, and then asked them about free will to which the child asked me what free will is and i had absolutely no response.
I had not prepared at all to try to explain what free will is to a child, but here i was.
The mom stepped in and said something to get me off the hook, but it was an eye opening first group.

The rest of the weekend flew by as i espoused about randomness to anyone who stopped by and listened.
I met tons of interesting people both visitors, and other project owners, but by the time Sunday midday roled around I was exhausted.
My project was such that I had to stand next to it and give a lecture for anyone to make any sense of it.
Without me there, there was not enough material on the door for anyone to get ANYTHING out of it if I was in the bathroom or just wanted a break.
I took my door home that week feeling super happy about the weekend, but also knowing I learned a millions lessons about educating a lay audience about technical topics.

Fast forward 10 months, and the steam plant is calling again for new projects.
I knew I wanted to participate again, and this year the theme was "break the system".
My mind immediately went to surveillance and anti-surveillance technology and all the cool ideas I could do hooking up surveillance cameras and showcasing how to get around face-id or gate detection.
That said, I know relatively little about those things, and determined it would be fun, but maybe a bit too much work.
I was reminded of the work of Georg Cantor and how when he introduced the notion of different sizes of infinity it, in some ways, broke the number system.
I was very happy with the alignment there and, wrote up an idea explaining number systems, and all the ways modern mathematicians have broken or modified it to suit their needs.

The project was accepted (note about how i was the only mathy project the first year) and again I waited until the week before to do any of the real planning.
This time, however, I did take the entire week before off work so I could give it a bit more the time it deserves.

I went with the door again since it worked well in the steam plant last year, and while i was getting the door set up design wise, I chatted with some other friends who I knew would be able to help me finalize the idea.
It immediately became clear that in the 12 months since the last fair, i had forgotten all the lessond learned because my project was again WAY over scoped.
I decided scoping the project down to 'just' the hilberts hotel thought experiment was enough, and still conveyed the large parts of what i was there to do: get people thinking about math and in particularly infinity.

The weekend went WAY smoother this time as I designed the project such that I could step away for periods of time to enjoy myself and the rest of the show.
It was so fun walking people through the thought experiment, but also watching people engage with the project on their own terms as i peeped from afar.
